Every year there's a new framework, a new language runtime, a new deployment paradigm. Most of them are genuinely interesting. Some of them are improvements. A smaller number will still be in production use five years from now.

> Choose boring technology. — Dan McKinley

I think about this constantly.

## The maintenance calculus

When you choose a new technology, you're not just choosing it for today — you're choosing it for the team that inherits the codebase in two years. For the on-call engineer paged at 2am who needs to understand what's happening. For the new hire who's never heard of this particular framework.

Boring technology has documentation. Stack Overflow answers. Battle-tested failure modes you can Google. It has people who've already made every mistake you're about to make and written about it.

## When to be interesting

Boring tech isn't always the answer. Sometimes a problem genuinely requires a new approach. Sometimes the productivity gain is large enough to justify the learning curve. Sometimes the team's excitement about a technology is itself a resource worth harnessing.

The question I try to ask is: *am I solving a problem or am I solving something interesting?* Both are valid, but they should be deliberate choices.

## The portfolio view

Not every decision has to be the safest decision. A useful mental model is to think of your tech choices as a portfolio. Most of the portfolio should be in reliable, boring assets. A small fraction can be in speculative bets.

If everything is a bet, nothing is stable. If nothing is a bet, you stop learning.
